Near-Earth Object (NEO) FAQ

What is a Near-Earth Object (NEO)?

NEOs are comets and asteroids that have been nudged by the gravitational attraction of nearby planets into orbits that allow them to enter the Earth’s neighborhood. Composed mostly of water ice with embedded dust particles, comets originally formed in the cold outer planetary system, while most rocky asteroids formed in the warmer inner solar system between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter.

How are the orbits of NEOs determined?

Scientists determine the orbit of an asteroid by comparing several different observations of its position in the sky over weeks or months. These observations are used to calculate the path of the object around the Sun.

What is a Potentially Hazardous Asteroid (PHA)?

A Potentially Hazardous Asteroid is defined based on parameters that measure the asteroid’s potential to make threatening close approaches to the Earth. Specifically, PHAs are asteroids with a minimum orbit intersection distance (MOID) of 0.05 au or less and an absolute magnitude (H) of 22.0 or brighter.

How do rovers generate power?

While asteroids drift through space, our rovers on Mars use Multi-Mission Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generators (MMRTGs). These convert heat from the natural decay of radioisotope fuel into electricity, providing constant power regardless of solar conditions.
